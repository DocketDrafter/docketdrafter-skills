# Business Records — CPLR 4518

## Issue

MCA funders move for summary judgment relying on payment histories, ACH
remittance logs, and bank records to prove the merchant breached the agreement.
These documents are hearsay unless they qualify under the CPLR 4518 business
records exception. The defense challenges the evidentiary foundation — attacking
the affiant's qualifications, the provenance of the records, and whether the
documents actually prove what the funder claims.

## Governing Rule

Under CPLR 4518(a), a business record is admissible if: (1) it was made in the
regular course of business; (2) it was the regular course of such business to
make it; and (3) it was made at or about the time of the event recorded (*People
v. Kennedy*). The affiant must have personal knowledge of the record-keeping
practices of the entity that created the record (*Palisades Collection v.
Kedik*; *Autovest v. Cassamajor*).

The critical wrinkle in MCA cases is that funders typically rely on records
created by third parties — banks, payment processors, prior servicers. Filing
someone else's records does not make them your business records (*People v.
Cratsley*). To admit third-party records, the affiant must either demonstrate
personal knowledge of the third party's record-keeping practices, or show the
records were incorporated into the recipient's own records and routinely relied
upon (*Bank of NY Mellon v. Gordon*).

## Context in MCA Cases

Most MCA funders submit payment histories generated by ACH processors or banks,
authenticated by an employee of the funder — not the bank. The recurring
questions include:

1. The affiant lacks personal knowledge of the **bank's or processor's**
   record-keeping practices (not just the funder's).
2. The funder has not shown the records were **incorporated into its own
   records** and routinely relied upon in its business.
3. The records are **internally created documents** that lack substantiation
   from the source institution.

Even if the court admits the records, the content may not prove breach — an R08
return code shows a payment was stopped but does not prove who stopped it or
that the defendant took affirmative action to interfere with collection.

*Bank of NY Mellon v. Gordon* is the strongest authority here — it is the 2d
Dept's comprehensive treatise on third-party business records, written by
Justice Miller to consolidate years of confused foreclosure-case law. Although
it arises in a mortgage context, its CPLR 4518 analysis applies directly to MCA
payment records. This topic remains supported mostly by general New York
evidence cases rather than MCA-specific appellate decisions, so the skill
should be explicit when drawing analogies from foreclosure or consumer-debt
collection contexts.

## Foundation Requirements / General Standard

- [People v. Kennedy](<../references/cases/courtlistener/People v. Kennedy.md>) — Court of Appeals; general standard for business records foundation under CPLR 4518
- [Winegrad v. New York University Medical Center](<../references/cases/courtlistener/Winegrad v. New York University Medical Center.md>) — Court of Appeals; movant must make prima facie showing with admissible evidence; failure requires denial regardless of opposing papers

## Affiant Must Have Personal Knowledge of Record-Keeping Practices

- [Palisades Collection, LLC v. Kedik](<../references/cases/courtlistener/Palisades Collection, LLC v. Kedik.md>) — 4th Dept; requires proof record made in regular course of business, contemporaneously, and authenticated by someone with knowledge of maker's practices
- [Autovest, LLC v. Cassamajor](<../references/cases/courtlistener/Autovest, LLC v. Cassamajor.md>) — 2d Dept; affiant must have personal knowledge of record-keeping practices

## Third-Party Records

- [Bank of New York Mellon v. Gordon](<../references/cases/justia/Bank of New York Mellon v. Gordon.md>), 2019 NY Slip Op 02306 (2d Dept. Mar. 27, 2019) — Comprehensive treatise on business records in foreclosure context; affiant must attest to personal knowledge of third party's record-keeping practices, or show records were incorporated into recipient's own records and routinely relied upon

## Documents Not Admissible Merely Because Retained in Party's Files

- [People v. Cratsley](<../references/cases/courtlistener/People v. Cratsley.md>) — Court of Appeals; documents not rendered admissible merely because retained in party's files

## Recurring Questions

- Whether the affiant is describing the creator's record-keeping practices or only the recipient's
- Whether third-party records were merely attached to motion papers or genuinely incorporated into the movant's own records
- Whether ACH logs, payment histories, and bank notices prove only that a debit failed, or also prove why it failed and who caused it
